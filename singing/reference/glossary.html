Singing Glossary

The shared vocabulary for this journey. Terms marked ★ are the load-bearing ones. New terms get added here as lessons introduce them.

A tenor is the highest standard male voice type — Pavarotti's voice. The famous "money notes" sit at the top of the tenor range.
Appoggio ★ /ah-POH-joh/ Lesson 1
Foundation
Italian, "to lean on". Breath support: keeping the inhalation posture as you sing out, so air is released slowly and the pressure under the cords stays steady. The single most important foundation; Pavarotti's recurring theme.
Diaphragm /DY-uh-fram/ Lesson 1
The dome-shaped breathing muscle below the lungs. It drops on inhale (belly moves out). In appoggio you slow its rise so breath lasts and stays even. You don't "push from the diaphragm" — you manage its return.
Bel canto /bel KAHN-toh/
"Beautiful singing" — the historical Italian school Pavarotti came from. Prizes even tone, smooth line (legato), breath support and natural placement over force and effects.
Support
Everyday English word for what appoggio describes — the controlled, steady breath pressure that "holds up" the tone. "Sing on the breath / on support."
Legato /leh-GAH-toh/
Smooth, connected singing — notes flowing into each other with no gaps or bumps. The "seamless" quality in a great aria line. (Coming in a later lesson.)
Passaggio /pah-SAH-joh/
The "passage" — the tricky transition zone between vocal registers (for tenors, roughly D4–A♭4). Where untrained high notes get shouty. We deliberately leave this for much later.
Squillo /SKWEEL-loh/
The bright, ringing "ping" that lets an operatic voice carry over an orchestra — part of why Pavarotti's high notes shine. A product of resonance, built long after the basics.
SOVT (semi-occluded vocal tract)
Exercises done through a partly-closed tract — humming, lip trills, straw phonation. Gentle, quiet, and great for healthy tone. Ideal for your modest-volume practice.
